Transport Equation Flow

The linear transport equation ∂tu + a(x)·∇u = 0 propagates initial data along characteristic curves — the integral curves of the velocity field a(x). Each characteristic is an orbit of the ODE ẋ = a(x), and the map sending initial positions to positions at time t is a diffeomorphism.

∂tu(t,x)  +  a(x) · ∇u(t,x)  = 0
u(t,x) — scalar field being transported
a(x) — velocity field (may be nonlinear)
Characteristics: ẋ = a(x(t)), u(t, x(t)) = const
Solution: u(t,x) = u₀(φ−t(x)) = u₀ ∘ φ−t
What is the transport equation?
Core Idea

Moving a quantity without changing its value

Imagine pouring dye into a flowing river. The dye is carried downstream, but the amount of dye at each fluid particle stays constant — it just moves to a new location. The transport equation is the mathematical description of exactly this.

The scalar field u(t,x) represents the quantity being carried (temperature, concentration, density). The vector field a(x) is the velocity that carries it. The PDE says: the total rate of change of u along any moving fluid particle is zero.

∂tu + a(x) · ∇u = 0   ⟺   d/dt u(t, x(t)) = 0

The left form is the Eulerian view (fixed coordinates). The right form is the Lagrangian view (following the particle) — and they are equivalent.

Physical Interpretation

What it models in the real world

The transport equation appears whenever a quantity is passively advected by a flow, with no diffusion and no sources. Real applications include:

  • Pollutant transport — concentration of a contaminant swept by wind or water currents
  • Color / dye advection — ink carried in a fluid, used to visualise flow fields
  • Kinetic theory — the Liouville equation governing probability density in phase space
  • Image warping — deforming a texture by a velocity field, used in computer vision and medical imaging
  • Shallow water / geophysics — passive scalar transport in ocean and atmosphere models
Solution Method

Method of characteristics — solving along curves

The key insight is that the PDE reduces to an ODE along special curves called characteristics. These are the trajectories of the velocity field.

Characteristic ODE:   ẋ(t) = a(x(t)),  x(0) = x₀
Solution formula:     u(t, x(t)) = u₀(x₀)

To find u at any point (t, x), you trace the characteristic backward from x to time 0, arriving at some initial point x₀. The answer is simply u₀(x₀) — the initial data at the foot of the characteristic.

This is the pull-back formula: u(t,x) = u₀(φ−t(x)), where φ−t integrates the ODE backward. The animation in Panel 2 computes exactly this for every pixel.

Diffeomorphism Connection

Why the flow map is a diffeomorphism

The flow map φt sends every initial point x₀ to its position x(t) under the ODE ẋ = a(x). For a smooth velocity field a(x), the Picard–Lindelöf theorem guarantees:

  • Existence & uniqueness — every point has exactly one characteristic through it
  • Smooth dependence — φt is C∞ in both t and x₀
  • Group law — φs ∘ φt = φs+t, so φ−t = (φt)−1

Together these mean φt is a bijection with smooth inverse — a diffeomorphism. The deformed blue grid in Panel 1 shows its image: smooth curves, no crossings, no folding.

det(Dφt) > 0 everywhere  ⟺  orientation-preserving diffeomorphism
Conservation & Jacobian — What changes, what doesn't

L∞ norm is preserved

Because u is constant along each characteristic, its maximum and minimum values never change. The sup-norm ‖u(t,·)‖∞ = ‖u₀‖∞ for all t. The animation shows this: the bright peak neither grows nor fades, it only moves.

L¹ norm and area distortion

The total "mass" ∫u dx is preserved only if div a = 0 (incompressible flow). For a general a(x), the Jacobian det(Dφt) ≠ 1, so volumes are distorted and the L¹ norm can change. Panel 6 in the earlier PDF showed exactly this.

Liouville's theorem

The Jacobian satisfies d/dt det(Dφt) = (div a)(φt) · det(Dφt). For the spiral field, div a = 2(1 − 2r²), which is negative near the limit cycle — the flow contracts area there, as you can see from the compressed grid cells.
